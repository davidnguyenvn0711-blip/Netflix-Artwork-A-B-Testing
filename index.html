01 · The Business Problem

Netflix Has a Thumbnail Problem

Every time you open Netflix, the platform makes thousands of invisible decisions. One of the most consequential: which thumbnail image should appear for each title? Netflix has found that the artwork displayed for a show or movie is one of the single biggest drivers of whether a user clicks to watch it — or scrolls past entirely.

This isn't a trivial design question. Netflix serves over 300 million subscribers in more than 190 countries. A thumbnail that increases clicks by even a few percentage points translates into millions of additional hours of content watched, stronger subscriber retention, and a direct lift in revenue. The challenge is knowing, before deploying an artwork change globally, whether the new design actually performs better — or whether any observed difference is just random noise.

That's where A/B testing comes in. Netflix runs hundreds of controlled experiments simultaneously, showing different artwork versions to randomly split groups of users and measuring which version drives more engagement. The version that wins statistically gets rolled out to everyone.

02 · The Method

What Is A/B Testing and Why Does It Matter?

An A/B test is a controlled experiment. You take a large group of users, split them randomly into two groups, show each group a different version of something — a webpage, an email subject line, a product price, an artwork thumbnail — and measure which version produces a better outcome.

The word "randomly" is doing enormous work here. Random assignment ensures the two groups are statistically equivalent before the experiment starts — same demographics, same viewing habits, same likelihood to click on anything. That means any difference in behavior you observe afterward can be attributed to the artwork itself, not to pre-existing differences between the groups.

A/B testing sits at the intersection of data science, machine learning, and business strategy. Netflix's experimentation platform doesn't just run tests — it feeds results back into machine learning models that learn which visual features (facial expressions, color palettes, scene composition) predict high engagement for which user segments. Over time, the system gets better at generating and selecting effective artwork automatically.

03 · The Experiment

What I Built and How It Works

Using Python, I simulated a Netflix-style A/B experiment from scratch — the same logical structure that Netflix's actual experimentation platform uses, built at a scale that can be inspected, audited, and explained step by step.

1,000 Simulated Users
2 Artwork Versions
24% Artwork A Click Rate
30% Artwork B Click Rate

Step 1 — Simulate the Experiment

Each of the 1,000 users is randomly assigned to either Artwork A or Artwork B with equal probability — a 50/50 split. Then, for each user, a simulated click is generated based on the true underlying probability for their group: 24% for Artwork A and 30% for Artwork B. The result is a clean three-column dataset with a user ID, their artwork assignment, and whether they clicked.

Step 2 — Measure Click-Through Rates

The data is grouped by artwork version and summarized: total users in each group, total clicks, and the click-through rate (CTR) — the fraction of users who clicked. A bar chart makes the difference immediately visible to any audience, regardless of their technical background.

Step 3 — Test for Statistical Significance

Observing that Artwork B has a higher CTR isn't enough. Any two groups will produce slightly different numbers by chance, even if the artwork has zero real effect. A two-proportion z-test calculates the probability (the p-value) of seeing a gap this large — or larger — if the two artworks were actually identical. A p-value below 0.05 means the result is statistically significant: we can be confident the difference is real, not a fluke.

Artwork B generated a 30% click-through rate versus 24% for Artwork A — a 6 percentage-point absolute difference and a 25% relative lift. The two-proportion z-test returned a p-value well below 0.05, confirming that this result is statistically significant. The difference is not random noise.

What "Statistically Significant" Actually Means

Statistical significance means the observed gap is unlikely to have occurred by chance alone. Specifically, a p-value below 0.05 means there is less than a 5% probability of seeing a difference this large if both artworks were truly equivalent. In business terms: we can be confident the artwork is causing the difference in clicks, not random variation in who happened to be in each group.

What a 25% Relative Lift Means at Netflix Scale

Netflix serves approximately 300 million subscribers globally. If a single artwork change produced a 25% relative improvement in click-through rate across even a fraction of those users, the downstream impact on hours streamed, subscriber satisfaction, and retention would be enormous. This is why Netflix considers its artwork personalization system one of its most valuable machine learning investments.

Step 1 · Simulate the Experiment
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.patches as mpatches
from scipy import stats

# Set seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)

# --- Simulation Parameters ---
n_users = 1000
p_a = 0.24  # Click probability for Artwork A
p_b = 0.30  # Click probability for Artwork B

# --- Step 1: Assign users to groups ---
user_ids = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
groups = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users, p=[0.5, 0.5])

# --- Step 2: Simulate clicks based on group assignment ---
clicks = np.where(
    groups == 'A',
    np.random.binomial(1, p_a, size=n_users),
    np.random.binomial(1, p_b, size=n_users)
)

# --- Step 3: Build the DataFrame ---
df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id': user_ids,
    'artwork_version': groups,
    'clicked': clicks
})
print(df.head(10))

# --- Step 4: Calculate summary metrics ---
summary = df.groupby('artwork_version')['clicked'].agg(
    total_users='count',
    total_clicks='sum',
    click_through_rate='mean'
).reset_index()

summary['click_through_rate'] = summary['click_through_rate'].round(4)

print("=" * 45)
print("        A/B TEST SUMMARY RESULTS")

ctr_a = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'A', 'click_through_rate'].values[0]
ctr_b = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'B', 'click_through_rate'].values[0]
lift = ((ctr_b - ctr_a) / ctr_a) * 100

Step 3 · Statistical Significance Test
# --- Step 6: Two-proportion z-test ---
clicks_a = df[df['artwork_version'] == 'A']['clicked'].sum()
clicks_b = df[df['artwork_version'] == 'B']['clicked'].sum()
n_a = df[df['artwork_version'] == 'A'].shape[0]
n_b = df[df['artwork_version'] == 'B'].shape[0]

# Pooled proportion under null hypothesis
p_pool = (clicks_a + clicks_b) / (n_a + n_b)
se = np.sqrt(p_pool * (1 - p_pool) * (1/n_a + 1/n_b))
z_stat = (ctr_b - ctr_a) / se
p_value = 2 * (1 - stats.norm.cdf(abs(z_stat)))

print("=" * 45)
print("     STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE TEST")
print("=" * 45)
print(f"Z-statistic : {z_stat:.4f}")
print(f"P-value     : {p_value:.4f}")
if p_value < 0.05:
    print("Result      : SIGNIFICANT — Artwork B wins.")
else:
    print("Result      : Not significant — insufficient evidence.")
print("=" * 45)

06 · Business Recommendation

What Netflix Should Do

Deploy Artwork B — With Conditions

The experiment produced a clear, statistically significant result: Artwork B outperforms Artwork A with a 25% relative lift in click-through rate and a p-value below 0.05. There is less than a 5% probability this result is due to chance. Based on this evidence, the recommendation is to deploy Artwork B as the default thumbnail for this title.

But Don't Stop There

Netflix's real system would layer several additional considerations on top of this finding before a full rollout:

Segment by user type. A thumbnail that wins on average may not win for every audience segment. Heavy drama viewers, casual weekend watchers, and mobile users may respond differently. Netflix would test whether Artwork B's lift holds across key segments before treating it as a universal winner.

Watch for long-term effects. A higher click-through rate only matters if it leads to actual viewing. If users click on Artwork B but quickly abandon the title, the artwork may be overpromising and underdelivering — which damages trust over time. Netflix measures completion rates and satisfaction scores, not just clicks.

Feed results into the personalization engine. The insight that certain visual features in Artwork B drove more clicks becomes a training signal for Netflix's machine learning models. Those models can then generate and rank future artwork options more effectively — creating a flywheel where each experiment makes the next one smarter.

The Broader Lesson

This project demonstrates a core principle of AI in business: intuition is not enough. A design team might have predicted that Artwork B would perform better — or they might have preferred Artwork A for aesthetic reasons. Neither preference matters without evidence. A/B testing replaces opinion with measurement and turns creative decisions into data-driven ones. That is what separates companies that experiment systematically from those that guess.
